# Inspiration

Games and ideas that shaped Crab Rustler's direction. This file is read by the Game Director and Feature Developer agents — treat it as the design compass, not a feature list.

---

## Vampire Survivors

**What Carl said:** The upgrade screen works in VS because you start to feel very powerful and fortunate when getting many chests at once. Here it feels more like pausing the game, which isn't as nice.

**What we're taking:** The reverse-VS premise is the whole game — instead of building character power, you build a conga train of caught crabs. The core tension (grow big for more reward vs. cash out safely) inverts VS's run structure. But VS's upgrade-screen-as-power-fantasy doesn't translate 1:1 here, so we space upgrades further apart and lean into the conga train as the source of "getting powerful."

**What we're *not* taking:** Constant upgrade interruptions that pause momentum. Crab Rustler's flow is rhythm-based, so breaks need to earn their interruption.

---

## Control (Remedy Entertainment)

**What Carl said:** The large level title screens look like Control's cool font aesthetic when getting to a new floor/level — but the levels don't vary or feel that impactful right now.

**What we're taking:** The *aesthetic* of Control's floor transitions — a bold, typographic moment that signals "you've crossed a threshold into somewhere new." Each level boundary should feel like arriving somewhere, not just a loading pause. Strong visual language, distinctive biome identity.

**What we're *not* taking (yet):** Control's density — we want fewer, longer, more impactful biome shifts rather than frequent small ones. The transition should be an event, not a rhythm hiccup.

---

## Diablo (series)

**What Carl said:** Procedurally generated levels in some form of horde mode / world record leaderboard setup like Diablo. "Easy to learn, hard to master" for longevity.

**What we're taking:** The arcade endless mode vision — procedural escalation, a leaderboard, runs that go as far as skill carries you. Not the RPG depth (no loot trees, no class system) — just the "keep going until you die" loop with a scoreboard to chase. The leaderboard is the prestige mechanic; every run is a question of how far.

**What we're *not* taking:** Complex meta-progression or build diversity. One game, one train, one score — the mastery is in reading the beat and arranging the conga.

---

## Precision platformers (Celeste, Super Meat Boy, etc.)

**What Carl said:** "If you do the mechanics PERFECTLY you can get really good and make it further" — like precision platformers, but around the beat mechanic really paying off.

**What we're taking:** A genuine skill ceiling. The beat system already has the scaffolding (PERFECT catches, groove meter, on-beat multipliers, combo chains). A player who hits every mechanic perfectly should score dramatically better than one who ignores the rhythm — not marginally better. The gap between "playing" and "playing well" should be legible and motivating.

**Direction for agents:** If a new mechanic doesn't have a skill-expressive version (a way to do it *better* that pays off noticeably), it's breadth not depth. Depth first.

---

## Inscryption (Daniel Mullins)

**What Carl said:** Fourth wall breaking game mechanics, like the desktop level idea.

**What we're taking:** The philosophy that the game can reach outside its own frame — that the most delightful moments are the ones that shouldn't be possible in a normal game. Specifically: a special level where the OS desktop is the terrain and player-moved windows reshape the level mid-run. Not documented. Just discovered. The player wonders "did the game mean for me to do that?" — and yes, it did.

**What we're *not* taking:** Inscryption's horror/narrative layer. This is a playful, party-game-energy version of the same idea. The fourth wall breaks with a wink, not a shudder.

---

## Old Windows desktop toys (Microsoft Power Toys era)

**What Carl said:** Those old Windows "power toys" games where the game lives on your desktop and interacts with your real windows and files.

**What we're taking:** The desktop-as-terrain level concept — transparent window, OS window borders as solid walls, crabs that navigate around real-world applications. The feeling that the game is "escaping" from its window. Technically ambitious but the surprise payoff for players who discover it is worth it.

---

## Design principles distilled from the above

1. **Rhythm over power fantasy.** The beat is the mechanic. Every system should make hitting the beat feel better, not be optional.
2. **Legible risk.** The long-train gamble should be visible (AT RISK tag, rope heat, escalating snap teeth) — players shouldn't lose crabs without understanding why it was their fault.
3. **Depth before breadth.** One mechanic done with a real skill ceiling beats three mechanics done shallowly.
4. **Thresholds, not interruptions.** Level transitions, upgrade moments, boss arrivals — each should feel earned and brief, not like a pause menu.
5. **Secrets that reward curiosity.** At least one mechanic (the desktop level) should be undocumented and discoverable. The best moment in the game might be one most players never find.
